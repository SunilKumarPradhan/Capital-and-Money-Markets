## 📊 Fundamental Concepts: Stocks vs Bonds

### What Are Stocks (Equities)?

> **Stock (Equity)**: A security that represents ownership in a corporation, entitling the holder to a proportionate share of the company's assets and earnings.

#### Key Characteristics of Stocks:

**1. Ownership Rights**
- When you buy stock, you own a piece of the company
- Stocks are called "equities" because they represent equity ownership
- Your ownership percentage = (Your shares / Total shares outstanding) × 100

**2. Voting Rights**
- Shareholders can vote on:
  - Election of board directors
  - Major corporate decisions
  - Mergers and acquisitions
  - Changes to corporate bylaws
- Voting can be done directly at shareholder meetings or by proxy

**3. Return Mechanisms**
- **Dividends**: Periodic cash payments from company profits
- **Capital Appreciation**: Increase in stock price over time
- No guaranteed returns (unlike bonds)

**4. Risk Profile**
- Higher risk than bonds
- Last in line during bankruptcy (after bondholders)
- Potential for total loss of investment
- Also potential for unlimited gains

#### Real-World Example:
```
If Apple has 16 billion shares outstanding and you own 1,600 shares:
- Your ownership = (1,600 / 16,000,000,000) × 100 = 0.00001%
- You can vote on corporate matters (proportional to your shares)
- You receive dividends when Apple declares them
- Your investment value fluctuates with Apple's stock price
```

---

### What Are Bonds (Fixed Income)?

> **Bond**: A debt security where the investor lends money to an entity (corporation or government) for a defined period at a fixed or variable interest rate.

#### Key Characteristics of Bonds:

**1. Lending Relationship**
- You are a creditor, not an owner
- The issuer (borrower) owes you money
- No ownership rights in the company

**2. Fixed Income Stream**
- Regular interest payments (called "coupon payments")
- Principal repayment at maturity
- Predictable cash flows (in most cases)

**3. Priority in Bankruptcy**
- Bondholders get paid before stockholders
- Secured bondholders have claims on specific assets
- Lower risk than stocks (generally)

**4. No Voting Rights**
- Cannot vote on corporate matters
- Cannot influence company decisions
- Relationship is purely contractual

#### Bond Structure Example:
```
Corporate Bond Details:
- Issuer: XYZ Corporation
- Face Value (Principal): $1,000
- Coupon Rate: 5% annually
- Maturity: 10 years
- Payment Frequency: Semi-annual

Cash Flows:
- Every 6 months: $25 interest payment ($1,000 × 5% ÷ 2)
- At maturity (Year 10): $1,000 principal + $25 final interest
- Total interest over 10 years: $500
- Total return: $1,500 ($1,000 principal + $500 interest)
```

### Stocks vs Bonds: Comprehensive Comparison

| Feature | Stocks | Bonds |
|---------|--------|-------|
| **Nature** | Ownership (Equity) | Loan (Debt) |
| **Returns** | Dividends + Capital Gains | Interest Payments |
| **Return Predictability** | Unpredictable | Predictable (if no default) |
| **Risk Level** | Higher | Lower |
| **Bankruptcy Priority** | Last (Residual claim) | First (Priority claim) |
| **Voting Rights** | Yes | No |
| **Maturity** | Perpetual (no end date) | Fixed maturity date |
| **Income Type** | Variable | Fixed (usually) |
| **Potential Returns** | Unlimited upside | Limited to interest + principal |
| **Potential Losses** | Can lose 100% | Can lose 100% (if default) |
| **Volatility** | High | Low to Moderate |
| **Suitable For** | Growth-seeking investors | Income-seeking investors |

## 💰 Bond Market Deep Dive

### Types of Bonds by Issuer

#### 1. Government Bonds (Sovereign Debt)

**US Treasury Bonds (Treasuries)**
- Issued by: US Federal Government
- Backed by: "Full faith and credit" of the US government
- Risk Level: Virtually risk-free (considered the safest)
- Why safest: Government can print money to repay debt
- Used as: Benchmark for risk-free rate in financial models

**Types of US Treasuries:**
```
By Maturity:
- Treasury Bills (T-Bills): < 1 year maturity
- Treasury Notes (T-Notes): 2-10 year maturity  
- Treasury Bonds (T-Bonds): 20-30 year maturity

Special Types:
- TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities): Principal adjusts with inflation
- I-Bonds: Savings bonds with inflation protection
```

**Other Government Bonds:**
- UK Gilts
- German Bunds
- Japanese Government Bonds (JGBs)
- Emerging market sovereign debt (higher risk)

#### 2. Municipal Bonds (Munis)

**Characteristics:**
- Issued by: State and local governments
- Purpose: Fund public projects (schools, highways, hospitals)
- Tax Advantage: Often exempt from federal (and sometimes state) income tax
- Risk: Higher than Treasuries, lower than corporate bonds
- Credit Rating: Varies by municipality's financial health

**Types:**
```
General Obligation Bonds:
- Backed by taxing power of the issuer
- Lower risk
- Used for general government purposes

Revenue Bonds:
- Backed by specific revenue streams (tolls, utility fees)
- Higher risk than GO bonds
- Used for specific projects
```

#### 3. Corporate Bonds

**Characteristics:**
- Issued by: Corporations
- Purpose: Raise capital for expansion, operations, acquisitions
- Risk: Higher than government bonds
- Returns: Higher yields to compensate for risk

**Credit Quality Categories:**

**Investment-Grade Bonds** (Lower Risk)
```
Rating Scale (S&P / Moody's):
AAA / Aaa - Highest quality, lowest risk
AA / Aa   - High quality
A / A     - Upper-medium grade
BBB / Baa - Lower-medium grade (lowest investment grade)

Characteristics:
- Lower default risk
- Lower yields
- Issued by financially stable companies
- Examples: Apple, Microsoft, Johnson & Johnson bonds
```

**High-Yield Bonds (Junk Bonds)** (Higher Risk)
```
Rating Scale:
BB / Ba and below - Speculative grade
CCC / Caa - Substantial risk
CC / Ca  - Highly speculative
C / C    - Near default
D        - In default

Characteristics:
- Higher default risk
- Higher yields (hence "high-yield")
- Issued by companies with weaker financials
- Greater price volatility
```

> **Why "Junk" Bonds?** The term "junk bond" refers to bonds rated below investment-grade (below BBB-/Baa3). Despite the negative connotation, they're also called "high-yield" bonds because they offer higher interest rates to compensate investors for the increased risk of default.

#### Real-World Example: Junk Bonds in 2008 Financial Crisis
```
During the 2008 crisis:
- Many corporate bonds were downgraded to junk status
- Companies like General Motors, Ford faced severe credit downgrades
- Junk bond yields spiked to 20%+ (vs. 5-7% normally)
- Many companies defaulted, leaving bondholders with losses
- Some investors who bought distressed bonds made huge profits when companies recovered
```

### The Fundamental Bond Pricing Rule

> **Core Principle**: Bond prices and interest rates move in OPPOSITE directions. When interest rates fall, bond prices rise. When interest rates rise, bond prices fall.

#### Why This Happens: The Mechanics

**Scenario 1: Interest Rates Fall**
```
Initial Situation:
- You own a bond paying 5% interest ($50/year on $1,000 face value)
- Market interest rates are 5%
- Your bond is worth $1,000 (par value)

Interest Rates Drop to 3%:
- New bonds now only pay 3% ($30/year)
- Your bond still pays 5% ($50/year)
- Your bond is now MORE attractive
- Investors will pay MORE than $1,000 to get your higher-paying bond
- Your bond price rises (might trade at $1,150, for example)
```

**Scenario 2: Interest Rates Rise**
```
Initial Situation:
- You own a bond paying 5% interest ($50/year on $1,000 face value)
- Market interest rates are 5%
- Your bond is worth $1,000 (par value)

Interest Rates Rise to 7%:
- New bonds now pay 7% ($70/year)
- Your bond still only pays 5% ($50/year)
- Your bond is now LESS attractive
- Investors will only pay LESS than $1,000 for your lower-paying bond
- Your bond price falls (might trade at $850, for example)
```

#### Mathematical Relationship

The bond pricing formula (simplified):
```
Bond Price = C/(1+r)¹ + C/(1+r)² + ... + (C+FV)/(1+r)ⁿ

Where:
C = Coupon payment
r = Market interest rate (yield)
FV = Face value
n = Number of periods

Key insight: As 'r' increases, the denominator increases, 
making each term smaller, thus reducing the bond price.
```

#### Practical Example with Numbers:
```python
# Bond Details
face_value = 1000
coupon_rate = 0.05
years_to_maturity = 10
annual_coupon = face_value * coupon_rate  # $50

# Scenario 1: Market rate = Coupon rate (5%)
market_rate_1 = 0.05
# Bond trades at par: $1,000

# Scenario 2: Market rate falls to 3%
market_rate_2 = 0.03
# Bond price rises to approximately: $1,170

# Scenario 3: Market rate rises to 7%
market_rate_3 = 0.07
# Bond price falls to approximately: $860

# The relationship is inverse and non-linear
```

#### Duration: Measuring Interest Rate Sensitivity

> **Duration**: A measure of how much a bond's price will change when interest rates change.

```
Higher Duration = Greater Price Sensitivity to Interest Rate Changes

Factors Affecting Duration:
1. Time to Maturity: Longer maturity → Higher duration
2. Coupon Rate: Lower coupon → Higher duration
3. Yield Level: Lower yields → Higher duration

Example:
- Bond with 5-year duration: If rates rise 1%, price falls ~5%
- Bond with 10-year duration: If rates rise 1%, price falls ~10%
```

## 📈 Risk-Return Relationship

### The Fundamental Trade-off

> **Risk-Return Trade-off**: The principle that potential return rises with an increase in risk. Higher-risk investments must offer higher expected returns to attract investors.

#### The Risk Pyramid

```
                    /\
                   /  \
                  /High\
                 /Return\
                /--------\
               / Stocks,  \
              /  Junk Bonds \
             /--------------\
            /  Investment-   \
           /   Grade Bonds    \
          /-------------------\
         /   Government Bonds  \
        /----------------------\
       /    Cash, T-Bills       \
      /--------------------------\
     
     Low Risk                High Risk
     Low Return              High Return
```

### Historical Evidence

**Long-term Average Annual Returns (1926-2020):**
```
Asset Class                 Average Return    Risk (Std Dev)
---------------------------------------------------------
Small-Cap Stocks                 16.2%            31.6%
Large-Cap Stocks                 12.2%            19.8%
Corporate Bonds                   6.4%             8.4%
Government Bonds                  5.5%             5.7%
Treasury Bills                    3.3%             3.1%
```

**Key Insights:**
1. Stocks outperform bonds over long periods
2. Higher returns come with higher volatility (risk)
3. Small-cap stocks have highest returns AND highest risk
4. T-Bills have lowest returns AND lowest risk

### Risk Types in Investments

#### 1. Market Risk (Systematic Risk)
- Affects entire market
- Cannot be diversified away
- Examples: Recession, interest rate changes, inflation
- Measured by: Beta (β)

#### 2. Company-Specific Risk (Unsystematic Risk)
- Affects individual companies
- CAN be diversified away
- Examples: Management changes, product failures, lawsuits
- Reduced by: Portfolio diversification

#### 3. Interest Rate Risk
- Bond prices fall when rates rise
- Affects fixed-income investments
- Measured by: Duration

#### 4. Credit Risk (Default Risk)
- Risk that borrower won't repay
- Higher for junk bonds
- Measured by: Credit ratings

#### 5. Liquidity Risk
- Risk of not being able to sell quickly
- Higher for small-cap stocks, corporate bonds
- Measured by: Bid-ask spread, trading volume
