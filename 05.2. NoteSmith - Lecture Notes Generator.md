## 📊 Volatility Swaps & Variance Swaps

<a name="what-are-volatility-swaps"></a>
### 2.1 What Are Volatility Swaps?

> **Volatility Swap**: A forward contract where the payoff depends on the realized volatility of an underlying asset, rather than its price direction.

#### The Core Concept

Think of traditional investing: you buy a stock hoping it goes **up**, or short it expecting it to go **down**. With volatility swaps, you're betting on something entirely different: **how much the price will move**, regardless of direction.

**Analogy**: 
- Traditional stock bet: "Will the temperature be hot or cold?"
- Volatility swap: "Will the temperature swing wildly between extremes, or stay stable?"

#### Why "Swap" Is Misleading

The term "swap" traditionally refers to exchanging cash flows (like fixed vs. floating interest rates). However:

- **Volatility swaps** are actually **forward contracts**
- **Variance swaps** (close cousins) are also forward contracts
- The payoff is determined by **realized variance** (how much the price actually fluctuated)

**Key Distinction**:
```
Traditional Swap: Exchange fixed rate for floating rate
Volatility Swap: Bet on realized volatility vs. strike volatility
```

<a name="payoff-calculation"></a>
### 2.2 Payoff Calculation & Mechanics

#### The Formula

```
Payoff = Notional Amount × (Realized Volatility - Volatility Strike)
```

**Components**:
1. **Notional Amount**: The size of the position (e.g., $1 million)
2. **Realized Volatility**: Actual volatility observed during the contract period
3. **Volatility Strike**: Predetermined volatility level agreed at contract start

#### Worked Example

**Setup**:
- Notional Amount: $1,000,000
- Volatility Strike: 20%
- Realized Volatility (at expiry): 25%

**Calculation**:
```python
# Volatility Swap Payoff Calculator

def calculate_volatility_swap_payoff(notional, realized_vol, strike_vol):
    """
    Calculate the payoff of a volatility swap.
    
    Parameters:
    notional (float): Notional amount in dollars
    realized_vol (float): Realized volatility as percentage (e.g., 25 for 25%)
    strike_vol (float): Strike volatility as percentage (e.g., 20 for 20%)
    
    Returns:
    float: Payoff amount (positive = profit, negative = loss)
    """
    # Convert percentages to decimals
    realized_vol_decimal = realized_vol / 100
    strike_vol_decimal = strike_vol / 100
    
    # Calculate payoff
    payoff = notional * (realized_vol_decimal - strike_vol_decimal)
    
    return payoff

# Example calculation
notional = 1_000_000  # $1 million
realized_vol = 25     # 25%
strike_vol = 20       # 20%

payoff = calculate_volatility_swap_payoff(notional, realized_vol, strike_vol)

print(f"Notional Amount: ${notional:,.0f}")
print(f"Volatility Strike: {strike_vol}%")
print(f"Realized Volatility: {realized_vol}%")
print(f"Payoff: ${payoff:,.0f}")

# Output:
# Notional Amount: $1,000,000
# Volatility Strike: 20%
# Realized Volatility: 25%
# Payoff: $50,000
```

**Result**: The buyer profits $50,000 because realized volatility exceeded the strike.

#### Payoff Profile

```
Profit/Loss
    ^
    |        /
    |       /
    |      /
    |     /
----+----/----------> Realized Volatility
    |   / Strike
    |  /
    | /
```

**Interpretation**:
- **Above Strike**: Buyer profits (seller loses)
- **Below Strike**: Buyer loses (seller profits)
- **Linear relationship**: Each 1% change in volatility = Notional × 1% change in payoff

<a name="real-world-applications"></a>
### 2.3 Real-World Applications

#### Who Uses Volatility Swaps?

1. **Hedge Funds**: 
   - Speculate on market volatility
   - Hedge portfolio volatility exposure
   - Example: Expecting market turbulence before elections

2. **Options Market Makers**:
   - Hedge vega risk (sensitivity to volatility changes)
   - Lock in volatility exposure

3. **Corporate Treasurers**:
   - Hedge currency or commodity price volatility
   - Budget certainty for international operations

4. **Pension Funds**:
   - Diversification strategy
   - Protection against market stress

#### When to Use Volatility Swaps

| Scenario | Action | Rationale |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| Expecting market crisis | Buy volatility swap | Volatility spikes during crises |
| Market too calm | Buy volatility swap | Mean reversion - volatility will increase |
| Overpriced options | Sell volatility swap | Implied volatility > expected realized |
| Stable market expected | Sell volatility swap | Collect premium in calm markets |

#### Real Example: 2008 Financial Crisis

During the 2008 crisis:
- VIX (volatility index) spiked from ~15% to 80%
- Volatility swap buyers with 20% strike made massive profits
- A $1M notional swap could have generated $600,000+ profit

## 🏛️ Basel III Regulatory Framework

<a name="origins-purpose"></a>
### 3.1 Origins & Purpose

#### The 2008 Crisis Catalyst

**What Went Wrong**:
- Banks held insufficient capital buffers
- Excessive leverage (30:1 or higher)
- Poor liquidity management
- Interconnected risks through OTC derivatives
- Lack of transparency

**The Response**: Basel III

> **Basel III**: A comprehensive set of reforms developed by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision to strengthen bank regulation, supervision, and risk management globally.

**Timeline**:
- **2008**: Financial crisis exposes vulnerabilities
- **2010**: Basel III framework published
- **2013-2019**: Phased implementation
- **2023**: Full implementation deadline (extended from 2019)

<a name="three-pillars"></a>
### 3.2 The Three Pillars of Basel III

#### Pillar 1: Minimum Capital Requirements

**Core Components**:

1. **Higher Capital Ratios**
   - Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1): Minimum 4.5% (up from 2%)
   - Tier 1 Capital: Minimum 6% (up from 4%)
   - Total Capital: Minimum 8%
   - Capital Conservation Buffer: Additional 2.5%

2. **Refined Risk-Weighted Assets (RWA) Calculation**
   - More stringent risk weights for different asset classes
   - Counterparty credit risk improvements
   - Trading book reforms

3. **New Liquidity Standards**

**Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR)**:
```
LCR = High-Quality Liquid Assets / Net Cash Outflows (30 days) ≥ 100%
```
- Ensures banks can survive 30-day stress scenario
- Must hold sufficient liquid assets

**Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR)**:
```
NSFR = Available Stable Funding / Required Stable Funding ≥ 100%
```
- Ensures stable funding over 1-year horizon
- Reduces maturity mismatch

#### Pillar 2: Supervisory Review Process

**Key Requirements**:

1. **Robust Corporate Governance**
   - Board oversight of risk management
   - Clear accountability structures
   - Independent risk functions

2. **Risk Concentration Management**
   - Limits on single-name exposures
   - Sector concentration monitoring
   - Geographic diversification

3. **Long-Term Incentive Alignment**
   - Compensation tied to long-term performance
   - Clawback provisions
   - Deferred bonuses

4. **Sound Compensation Practices**
   - Avoid excessive risk-taking incentives
   - Balance between fixed and variable pay

5. **Supervisory Colleges**
   - Cross-border coordination for global banks
   - Information sharing between regulators

6. **Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment Process (ICAAP)**
   - Banks assess their own capital needs
   - Stress testing requirements
   - Scenario analysis

7. **Supervisory Review and Evaluation Process (SREP)**
   - Regulators evaluate bank assessments
   - Impose additional capital if needed

#### Pillar 3: Market Discipline

**Enhanced Disclosure Requirements**:

1. **Risk Management Practices**
   - Risk governance structure
   - Risk appetite framework
   - Risk measurement methodologies

2. **Capital Adequacy**
   - Capital ratios and components
   - RWA calculations
   - Leverage ratio

3. **Securitization Exposures**
   - Detailed breakdown of securitization positions
   - Risk retention requirements
   - Due diligence processes

**Purpose**: Enable market participants to assess bank risk profiles and make informed decisions.

<a name="ccps"></a>
### 3.3 Central Counterparty Clearing Houses (CCPs)

#### What Is a CCP?

> **Central Counterparty (CCP)**: An entity that interposes itself between counterparties to trades, becoming the buyer to every seller and seller to every buyer.

**Before CCP** (Bilateral Trading):
```
Bank A ←→ Bank B
Bank A ←→ Bank C
Bank B ←→ Bank C
```
Each bank faces counterparty risk from every other bank.

**After CCP**:
```
Bank A ←→ CCP ←→ Bank B
         ↕
       Bank C
```
All banks face only the CCP, which manages the risk.

#### Basel III Incentives for CCPs

**Qualifying Central Counterparty (QCCP)** Benefits:

| Aspect | Bilateral Trading | QCCP (e.g., Eurex Clearing) |
|--------|-------------------|------------------------------|
| **Risk Weight** | 20%-100% | **2%** |
| **Capital Required** | High | **Very Low** |
| **Netting** | Bilateral only | **Multilateral** |
| **Default Risk** | Direct exposure | **Waterfall protection** |

#### Example: Eurex Clearing

**Risk Weight Impact**:

```python
# Capital Requirement Comparison

def calculate_capital_requirement(exposure, risk_weight, capital_ratio=0.08):
    """
    Calculate regulatory capital requirement.
    
    Parameters:
    exposure (float): Exposure amount in dollars
    risk_weight (float): Risk weight as decimal (e.g., 0.02 for 2%)
    capital_ratio (float): Minimum capital ratio (default 8%)
    
    Returns:
    float: Required capital
    """
    rwa = exposure * risk_weight
    capital_required = rwa * capital_ratio
    return capital_required

# Example: $100 million derivative exposure
exposure = 100_000_000

# Bilateral trading (assume 100% risk weight)
bilateral_capital = calculate_capital_requirement(exposure, 1.0)

# QCCP clearing (2% risk weight)
qccp_capital = calculate_capital_requirement(exposure, 0.02)

# Savings
capital_savings = bilateral_capital - qccp_capital
savings_percentage = (capital_savings / bilateral_capital) * 100

print(f"Exposure: ${exposure:,.0f}")
print(f"\nBilateral Trading:")
print(f"  Risk Weight: 100%")
print(f"  Capital Required: ${bilateral_capital:,.0f}")
print(f"\nQCCP Clearing (Eurex):")
print(f"  Risk Weight: 2%")
print(f"  Capital Required: ${qccp_capital:,.0f}")
print(f"\nCapital Savings: ${capital_savings:,.0f} ({savings_percentage:.0f}%)")

# Output:
# Exposure: $100,000,000
