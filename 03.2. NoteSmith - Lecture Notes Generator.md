## 🏭 Crude Oil Distillation Process

### What is Distillation?

> **Distillation** is the physical separation of crude oil into different fractions based on their boiling points. It's the foundational process that transforms "black gold" into usable products.

### Why Distillation Exists
Crude oil in its raw form is a complex mixture of hundreds of different hydrocarbon molecules. Each has different:
- Boiling points
- Molecular weights
- Commercial values
- End uses

Distillation allows refiners to separate these components and maximize economic value.

### How the Distillation Tower Works

```
Temperature Gradient in Distillation Tower:

TOP (Coolest ~40°C)
    ↑
    | Fuel Gas (lightest, lowest boiling point)
    |
    | Naphtha
    |
    | Gasoline
    |
    | Kerosene / Jet Fuel
    |
    | Diesel / Gas Oil
    |
    | Heavy Gas Oil
    ↓
BOTTOM (Hottest ~400°C)
    Residue (heaviest, highest boiling point)
```

**Step-by-Step Process:**

1. **Heating**: Crude oil is heated to ~350-400°C in a furnace
2. **Vaporization**: Most components turn into vapor
3. **Tower Entry**: Hot vapor enters the distillation column at the bottom
4. **Rising & Cooling**: Vapor rises through the tower, cooling as it ascends
5. **Condensation**: Each fraction condenses when it reaches its specific boiling point
6. **Collection**: Trays at different heights collect different fractions
7. **Residue**: Heavy components that don't vaporize remain at the bottom

### The Science Behind It

**Boiling Point Principle:**
- Small molecules (like propane) have weak intermolecular forces → low boiling points → condense at top
- Large molecules (like heavy fuel oil) have strong intermolecular forces → high boiling points → remain at bottom

**Real-World Application:**
A typical refinery processes 100,000-500,000 barrels per day through multiple distillation units running 24/7. The Jamnagar Refinery in India, the world's largest, can process 1.24 million barrels daily.

## ⚗️ Cracking: Transforming Heavy to Light

### What is Cracking?

> **Cracking** is the chemical process of breaking large hydrocarbon molecules into smaller, more valuable ones through heat, pressure, and/or catalysts.

### Why Cracking is Essential

**The Economic Problem:**
- Distillation alone produces ~20% gasoline from crude oil
- Market demand for gasoline is ~45-50% of total refined products
- Heavy fuel oil has lower value and declining demand

**The Solution:**
Cracking converts low-value heavy fractions into high-value light products.

### Types of Cracking

| Type | Method | Temperature | Pressure | Products | Use Case |
|------|--------|-------------|----------|----------|----------|
| **Thermal Cracking** | Heat only | 450-750°C | High (70 bar) | Gasoline, diesel, olefins | Older technology, less common |
| **Catalytic Cracking (FCC)** | Heat + Catalyst | 480-550°C | Low (2-3 bar) | High-octane gasoline | Most common in refineries |
| **Hydrocracking** | Heat + Hydrogen + Catalyst | 260-425°C | Very high (100-200 bar) | Diesel, jet fuel | Premium products |

### The Chemistry Simplified

**Before Cracking:**
```
C₂₀H₄₂ (Heavy Gas Oil - 20 carbon atoms)
Large molecule, low value
```

**After Cracking:**
```
C₈H₁₈ (Octane - gasoline component) + C₆H₁₄ (Hexane) + C₄H₈ (Butene - plastic feedstock) + C₂H₄ (Ethylene)
Multiple smaller molecules, higher total value
```

### Real-World Example

**Input:** 100 barrels of heavy gas oil @ $60/barrel = $6,000
**Output after cracking:**
- 45 barrels gasoline @ $90/barrel = $4,050
- 30 barrels diesel @ $80/barrel = $2,400
- 15 barrels jet fuel @ $85/barrel = $1,275
- 10 barrels petrochemical feedstock @ $70/barrel = $700

**Total output value:** $8,425
**Value added by cracking:** $2,425 (40% increase!)

### When to Use Cracking

**Use cracking when:**
- Gasoline demand exceeds natural distillation yield
- Heavy fuel oil prices are depressed
- Petrochemical feedstock demand is high
- Refinery has access to cheap hydrogen (for hydrocracking)

**Don't crack when:**
- Heavy fuel oil prices are strong (rare)
- Cracking unit maintenance costs exceed margin
- Environmental regulations make it uneconomical

## 🛢️ Refined Oil Products (From Heavy to Light)

### The "Cut" of the Barrel

Understanding where each product sits in the distillation tower helps predict pricing relationships.

### 1️⃣ Fuel Oil (Heaviest)

**Properties:**
- Boiling point: >340°C
- Viscosity: Very high (thick, syrupy)
- Color: Dark brown to black
- Sulfur content: Variable (0.5% to 3.5%)

**Uses:**
- Power generation (declining)
- Marine shipping fuel
- Industrial heating and boilers
- Asphalt production (from residue)

**Market Dynamics:**
- **Declining demand**: Shift to natural gas and renewables
- **Environmental pressure**: IMO 2020 regulations limited sulfur to 0.5% for marine fuel
- **Price sensitivity**: High-sulfur fuel oil (HSFO) trades at significant discount to low-sulfur fuel oil (LSFO)

**Pricing Example:**
```
HSFO (3.5% sulfur): $400/ton
LSFO (0.5% sulfur): $550/ton
Discount for high sulfur: $150/ton (27%)
```

**Real-World Application:**
After IMO 2020, many shipping companies installed "scrubbers" (exhaust cleaning systems) to continue using cheaper HSFO, while others switched to LSFO or LNG fuel.

---

### 2️⃣ Gas Oil / Diesel (Middle Distillates)

**Properties:**
- Boiling point: 250-350°C
- Viscosity: Medium
- Color: Clear to light amber
- Sulfur content: <10 ppm (ultra-low sulfur diesel)

**Uses:**
- Transportation (trucks, buses, cars in Europe)
- Off-road equipment (construction, agriculture)
- Heating oil (residential and commercial)
- Backup generators

**Market Dynamics:**
- **Seasonal demand**: Heating oil demand spikes in winter (Northern Hemisphere)
- **Diesel premium**: Often trades at premium to gasoline in Europe and Asia
- **Cetane rating**: Quality measure (like octane for gasoline)

**Regional Differences:**
- **Europe**: 60% diesel vehicles → high diesel demand
- **USA**: 20% diesel vehicles → more gasoline demand
- **Asia**: Growing diesel demand for transportation and industry

---

### 3️⃣ Jet Fuel / Kerosene (Middle Distillates)

**Properties:**
- Boiling point: 150-250°C
- Viscosity: Low to medium
- Flash point: >38°C (safety requirement)
- Freeze point: <-40°C (high altitude requirement)

**Uses:**
- Commercial aviation (Jet A, Jet A-1)
- Military aviation (JP-8)
- Space heating (kerosene heaters)
- Lighting (developing countries)

**Market Dynamics:**
- **Highly cyclical**: Tied to air travel demand
- **COVID-19 impact**: Demand collapsed 50%+ in 2020
- **Quality premium**: Strict specifications command premium pricing

**Pricing Benchmark:**
Singapore Jet Fuel is the key Asian benchmark, while US Gulf Coast Jet Fuel is the American benchmark.

---

### 4️⃣ Naphtha (Light)

**Properties:**
- Boiling point: 30-200°C
- Volatility: High
- Color: Clear
- Composition: C5-C12 hydrocarbons

**Uses:**
- **Primary**: Petrochemical feedstock (plastics production)
- **Secondary**: Gasoline blending component
- Solvent in chemical industry

**Market Dynamics:**
- **Petrochemical link**: Price correlates with ethylene and propylene
- **Regional variation**: Asia imports heavily for petrochemical complexes
- **Substitution**: Competes with ethane and LPG as cracker feedstock

**Value Chain:**
```
Naphtha → Steam Cracker → Ethylene → Polyethylene → Plastic products
                        → Propylene → Polypropylene → Packaging, automotive parts
```

---

### 5️⃣ Gasoline (Lightest Liquid Product)

**Properties:**
- Boiling point: 40-200°C
- Volatility: Very high
- Octane rating: 87-95 (regular to premium)
- Composition: C4-C12 hydrocarbons

**Uses:**
- Light-duty vehicles (cars, motorcycles)
- Small engines (lawnmowers, generators)
- Racing fuel (high-octane variants)

**Octane Rating Explained:**

> **Octane rating** measures gasoline's resistance to engine knocking (premature combustion). Higher octane allows higher compression ratios and better performance.

| Grade | Octane (RON) | Use Case | Price Premium |
|-------|--------------|----------|---------------|
| Regular | 87-91 | Standard vehicles | Base price |
| Mid-grade | 89-93 | Some SUVs, performance cars | +$0.20-0.30/gallon |
| Premium | 91-95 | High-performance, luxury, turbocharged | +$0.40-0.60/gallon |
| Racing | 100+ | Motorsports | +$2-5/gallon |

**Market Dynamics:**
- **Seasonal patterns**: Summer driving season increases demand
- **Reformulation**: Summer vs. winter blends (vapor pressure regulations)
- **Regional specs**: California has strictest environmental requirements

**Real-World Example:**
A refiner might produce:
- 60% regular gasoline (87 octane)
- 25% mid-grade (89 octane)
- 15% premium (93 octane)

The octane premium allows them to capture additional margin from premium sales.

---

### 6️⃣ Fuel Gas (Lightest)

**Properties:**
- Boiling point: <0°C
- State: Gas at room temperature
- Composition: C1-C4 (methane, ethane, propane, butane)

**Uses:**
- Refinery fuel (powers the refinery itself)
- Petrochemical feedstock
- LPG (propane/butane mix) for heating and cooking

**Market Dynamics:**
- Often consumed internally by refineries
- Excess sold as LPG
- Valuable as petrochemical feedstock

## 💰 Crack Spreads & Refiner Economics

### What is a Crack Spread?

> **Crack spread** is the difference between the price of crude oil (input) and the combined price of refined products (outputs). It represents the gross refining margin.

### Why It's Called "Crack" Spread
The term comes from "cracking" crude oil into products. It's the refiner's profit indicator.

### The Basic Economics

**Simplified Refinery Economics:**
```
Revenue (Products) - Cost (Crude Oil) - Operating Costs = Refining Margin
```

**Crack Spread Formula:**
```
Crack Spread = (Price of Products) - (Price of Crude Oil)
```

### Common Crack Spread Ratios

| Ratio | Name | Calculation | Use Case |
|-------|------|-------------|----------|
| **1:1** | Simple Crack | 1 barrel product - 1 barrel crude | Basic margin indicator |
| **3:2:1** | Gulf Coast Crack | (2 gasoline + 1 diesel) - 3 crude | US refinery benchmark |
| **5:3:2** | Complex Crack | (3 gasoline + 2 diesel) - 5 crude | More realistic product mix |
| **2:1:1** | Heating Oil Crack | (1 gasoline + 1 heating oil) - 2 crude | Northeast US winter |

### Real-World Example: 3:2:1 Crack Spread

**Scenario:**
- WTI Crude Oil: $80/barrel
- RBOB Gasoline: $2.50/gallon = $105/barrel (42 gallons/barrel)
- ULSD Diesel: $2.40/gallon = $100.80/barrel

**Calculation:**
```
Output value: (2 × $105) + (1 × $100.80) = $310.80
Input cost: 3 × $80 = $240
Crack Spread: $310.80 - $240 = $70.80 per 3 barrels
Per barrel margin: $70.80 ÷ 3 = $23.60/barrel
```

**Interpretation:**
A $23.60/barrel crack spread is healthy. Refiners typically need $10-15/barrel to cover operating costs, so this represents strong profitability.
